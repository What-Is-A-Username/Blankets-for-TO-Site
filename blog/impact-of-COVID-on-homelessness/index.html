Impact of COVID on homelessness

COVID

Awareness

Article

by Zaynab Azeem

February 6th, 2022

The pandemic has had a drastic impact on everyone: students, educators, businesses, and jobs. However, there is one community that has been the most affected, but is often overlooked - the homeless. Homelessness was already on the rise since before the pandemic, but ever since late 2019, homelessness rates have risen dramatically. It was reported that during the harsh winters of January 2021, there were an additional 30,000 cases of bankruptcy and homelessness reported.

Every year, an estimated 235,000 Canadians find themselves homeless. These Canadians are among the most vulnerable, with no practical option to adopt social distancing in order to avoid contracting COVID-19, which adds to the list of reasons why homelessness is a national emergency. Moreover, according to a study done by Lucie Richard, recent homeless individuals are more susceptible to COVID hospitalizations, 10 times more likely to get administered in an intensive care unit, and 5 times more likely to die within 21 days of a positive test. Dr. Richards research also found that with Ontarios increased frequency of testing, the homeless population had 75% more positive tests than the population with housing.

Although the government has tried to provide these vulnerable communities with shelters, there has been less effort made to ensure social distancing, and according to a study done by Dr. Salimah Shariff, the high population density and morbidity of these shelters have put these individuals at a higher risk. While most people advocate for the increase of shelters for the homeless during these harsh times, Dr. Andrew Boozary, the Executive Director of Social Medicine at the University Health Network says otherwise. According to him, the air circulation and living conditions of these shelters are extremely formidable, and it would be better for the homeless community to take refuge in tents.

One of the solutions to this problem is the potential increase in hotel recovery sites being set up in some municipalities. According to Boozary, housing and health go hand in hand as providing appropriate separated refuge has become a mandate in such conditions. Another important part of bridging this gap is to make homeless people feel welcome by creating trust between them and the communities that assist them, and that is exactly what we strive to do here at Blankets for Toronto.
